A baníg is a handwoven mat usually used in East Asia and the Philippines for sleeping and sitting. This type of mat is traditionally made in the Philippines.

Technically, it is not a textile. Depending on the region of the Philippines, the mat is made of buri[1] (palm), pandanus or sea grass leaves. The leaves are dried, usually dyed, then cut into strips and woven into mats, which may be plain or intricate.[2]

The Samal of Sulu usually make their mats out of buri leaves. Mats from Basey, Samar use tikog leaves which are dyed in strong colours to make beautiful, unique designs. Banig mats from Bukidnon are made from sodsod grass, a ribless reed endemic to the area.

Banig's definition

In East Asia and the Philippines, a banig is a handwoven mat that is commonly used for sleeping and sitting. The Philippines has a long history of making this style of mat. Despite the fact that it has been more commonly utilized.

It isn't a textile in the strictest sense. The mat is composed of buri (palm), pandan, or sea grass leaves, depending on where you are in the Philippines. It's a simple or intricate mat that's woven out of dried leaves that have been coloured and chopped into strips.

Basey, Samar is another region in the Philippines that is known for its beautifully made mats. The banig in Basey, unlike Sulu's, are fashioned of tikog leaves. To create stunning, vibrant, and one-of-a-kind designs, the leaves are dyed with a variety of powerful hues.
