16. 4. DAMATH is a board game incorporated mathematical skills in the Filipino game Dama. In a school DAMATH tournament, there are 28 participants who are divided into 7 groups. Each participant plays against each member of his group in the eliminations. The winner in each group advances to the semi-finals where they again complete with each other. The five players with most number of wins proceed to the final round and play against each other. Assume that there are no ties. a. What is the total number of games to be played in the eliminations? b. How many matches will be played in the final around? c. In how many possible ways can the top five players in the semi-finals come up? d. In how many possible ways can the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd placer be declared in the final round? e. How many matches will be played altogether?


Answer:

a. 42

b. 10

c. 21

d. 60

e. 73

Step-by-step explanation:

Before we answer these questions, let us first discuss the concept of permutation and combination.

Permutation is arranging things from a possible pool of things, into an order. The keyword for permutation is order. The way how you arrange things is important when it comes to permutation.

Combination is also an arrangement of things from a possible selection. The difference is that the order at which things are arranged does not matter in a combination.

A good example of permutation is arranging students in a line. An example of using combination is forming a committee. The order matters in the first scenario of arranging students in a line. In a committee, it does not matter whether a person is selected first or second, but if that person is in that committee or not.

Permutation and combination have different formulas.

For permutation, the number of ways to arrange r things from n possible things, when the order matters is

[tex]\frac{n!}{(n-r)!}[/tex]

For combination, the number of ways to arrange r things from n possible things, with no regard to order is

[tex]\frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}[/tex]

We need these concepts to solve the problem.

a.

In the elimination round there are 28 participants divided into 7 groups. This means there are 4 persons in a group. DAMATH is played between two players. The number of matches in a group is the same as choosing 2 people to play DAMATH from 4 possible players. We do not care about the order here because Player 1 playing Player 2 is the same as Player 2 playing Player 1.

Substituting n = 4, and r = 2 to the combination formula gives us

[tex]\frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}\\\\\frac{4!}{2!(4-2)!}\\\\\frac{4*3*2}{2!2!}\\\\\frac{4*3*2}{2*2}\\\\6[/tex]

There are 6 matches in 1 group. We have 7 groups.

[tex]6*7 = 42[/tex]

There will be 42 matches in the elimination round.

b.

In the final round, there are 5 remaining players. We do the same thing as we did with letter a. There are 5 players and 2 are needed to play DAMATH.

Substituting n = 4, and r = 2 to the combination formula gives us

[tex]\frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}\\\\\frac{5!}{2!(5-2)!}\\\\\frac{5!}{2!3!}\\\\\frac{5*4*3!}{2*3!}\\\\10[/tex]

There will be 10 games in the final round.

c.

The winner of each group from the elimination advances to the semi-finals. There are 7 groups, and 1 winner from each group. This means that there are 7 semi-finalists. From this 7, 5 will advance to the finals. We choose 5 finalists from a possible 7 semi-finalists.

Substituting n = 7, and r = 5 to the combination formula gives us

[tex]\frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}\\\\\frac{7!}{5!(7-5)!}\\\\\frac{7!}{5!2!}\\\\\frac{7*6*5!}{5!*2}\\\\7*3\\\\21[/tex]

There are 21 ways for to choose 5 finalists from the 7 semi-finalists.

d.

In the final round, there are 5 finalists. There are 3 awards, 1st, 2nd, and 3rd place. We are choosing 3 winners from 5 finalists. However, the order matters because a player winning 1st is different from the same player winning 3rd place.

Substituting n = 5, and r = 3 to the permutation formula gives us:

[tex]\frac{n!}{(n-r)!}\\\\\frac{5!}{(5-3)!}\\\\\frac{5!}{2!}\\\\\frac{5*4*3*2!}{2!}\\\\5*4*3\\\\60[/tex]

There are 60 ways for three awardees to be declared in the final round.

e.

Earlier (from a), we know that we have 42 elimination matches.

In the semis, there are 7 people and 2 are needed to play DAMATH. Like what we did earlier in a) and b) we can substitute n = 7, r = 2 in the combination formula.

[tex]\frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}\\\\\frac{7!}{2!(7-2)!}\\\\\frac{7*6*5!}{2!5!}\\\\7*3\\21[/tex]

There are 21 semi-final matches.

Earlier (from b), we know that we have 10 games in the finals.

Adding them all together gives us:

[tex]42+21+10 = 73[/tex]

There are 73 matches altogether in the competition.
